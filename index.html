Sustainable Development
Definition

"Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

— Brundtland Commission, 1987
Why Sustainable Development is Needed

Earlier development focused on: industrial growth, infrastructure expansion, profit maximization, and rapid urbanization — which caused serious problems.

Problems Caused by Traditional Development
ProblemEffect
PollutionAir, water and soil degradation
Climate ChangeGlobal warming and extreme weather
Resource DepletionExhaustion of water, coal, oil
Biodiversity LossSpecies extinction
Social InequalityRich-poor gap
Core Idea

Development should be: Balanced · Responsible · Long-Term · Inclusive

Three Pillars of Sustainable Development
Environmental

Protection and conservation of natural resources.

  • Pollution control
  • Biodiversity conservation
  • Forest protection
  • Renewable energy usage
Example

Solar power instead of coal power.

Economic

Economic growth without environmental destruction.

  • Job creation
  • Innovation
  • Resource efficiency
  • Long-term growth
Example

Green manufacturing industries.

Social

Improving quality of life and ensuring equality.

  • Poverty reduction
  • Education & healthcare
  • Gender equality
  • Equal opportunities
Example

Affordable education for all citizens.

Curbing Climate Change

Long-term alteration in temperature, rainfall, wind patterns, and other climatic conditions due to natural and human activities.

Weather vs Climate

Weather

  • Short-term atmospheric condition
  • Changes hourly/daily
  • Forecast for days
  • "It is raining today."

Climate

  • Long-term average weather pattern
  • Changes over decades
  • Pattern over 30+ years
  • "Kerala receives heavy rainfall every year."
Earth's Energy Balance
Process
☀ Incoming Solar Radiation (Visible light + UV)
↓
Reflection by Clouds, Ice, Snow (Albedo)
↓
Absorption by Land, Oceans, Forests
↓
Earth emits Infrared Radiation (Heat)
Stable

Incoming = Outgoing → Stable Temperature

Global Warming

Incoming > Outgoing → Global Warming

Greenhouse Effect
How It Works
1. Sunlight reaches Earth
↓
2. Earth absorbs energy
↓
3. Earth emits infrared heat
↓
4. Greenhouse gases trap heat
Natural vs Enhanced
Natural

Earth Temperature ≈ 15°C — Life exists

Without GHG

Earth Temperature ≈ −18°C — Life would not exist

Enhanced (Human-caused)

More heat trapped → Global warming → Climate change

Consequences of Climate Change
Environmental Impacts
  • Loss of Biodiversity (Arctic species losing ice habitats)
  • Coral Reef Bleaching (Great Barrier Reef)
  • Forest Fires (drought + high temperatures)
  • Desertification (fertile land → desert)
  • Unpredictable rainfall, floods, droughts
Water Resource Impacts
  • Glacier Retreat (Himalayan glaciers)
  • Groundwater Depletion
  • Floods (property damage, contamination)
  • Droughts (water shortages, crop failure)
  • Drinking Water Scarcity
Agricultural Impacts
  • Reduced Crop Yield (excess heat)
  • Crop Failure (heat stress)
  • Pests and Diseases (warmer climate)
  • Food Insecurity (reduced availability + higher prices)
Human Health Impacts
  • Heat Stroke & Dehydration
  • Vector-Borne Diseases (Malaria, Dengue, Chikungunya)
  • Malnutrition (food shortage)
  • Respiratory Diseases (Asthma, Bronchitis)
Economic Impacts
  • Infrastructure Damage (roads, bridges, buildings)
  • Loss of Livelihoods (agriculture, fisheries, tourism)
  • Disaster Recovery Costs
  • Climate Refugees
Coastal Impacts
  • Sea Level Rise → Coastal flooding, Displacement
  • Coastal Erosion (shorelines retreating)
  • Saltwater Intrusion → Unsafe drinking water, Reduced agriculture

Saving Biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety and variability of life on Earth, including plants, animals, microorganisms, and the ecosystems they form.

Levels of Biodiversity
1. Genetic Diversity

Variation in genes within the same species.

Examples

Different rice varieties · Human genetic variations · Different cattle breeds

  • Adaptation to environmental changes
  • Disease resistance
  • Evolutionary potential
2. Species Diversity

Variety of different species present in a region.

Examples

Forest ecosystems · Coral reefs · Grasslands

  • Ecological balance
  • Food chain stability
  • Healthy ecosystem
3. Ecosystem Diversity

Variety of ecosystems found on Earth.

Examples

Forests · Deserts · Wetlands · Grasslands · Oceans

  • Different habitats
  • Nutrient cycling
  • Supports multiple life forms

Biodiversity Under Threat
1. Habitat Loss

Destruction or fragmentation of natural habitats.

UrbanizationAgricultureMiningInfrastructure
Example

Deforestation for agriculture → Species displacement & extinction risk.

2. Climate Change
  • Species migration
  • Coral bleaching
  • Altered breeding cycles
  • Species extinction
Example

Polar species losing habitats.

3. Pollution
Acid rainOil spillsHeavy metalsMarine plastic waste

Effects: Toxic environments · Species decline

4. Overexploitation

Excessive use of natural resources beyond regeneration capacity.

OverfishingHuntingPoachingExcessive plant harvesting

Effects: Resource depletion · Food chain imbalance

5. Invasive Species

Non-native species introduced into an ecosystem.

  • Competition with native species
  • Spread of diseases
  • Ecosystem disruption
Example

Water Hyacinth in Indian water bodies.

Effects of Biodiversity Loss
  • Ecosystem Imbalance
  • Food chain collapse
  • Loss of Food Security
  • Reduced Ecosystem Services (Pollination, Water purification, Soil fertility)
  • Species Extinction (permanent loss)
Oceans & Fisheries
Key Fact

Oceans cover approximately 70% of Earth's surface.

Functions of Oceans
  • Absorb heat, regulate temperature, absorb CO₂
  • Food Resources: Fish, Shellfish, Seaweed
  • Marine Ecosystems: Coral reefs, Mangroves
  • Economic: Tourism, Fisheries, Shipping, Energy
Types of Fisheries
Marine Fisheries

Oceans & Seas — Examples: Tuna, Sardines, Prawns

Inland Fisheries

Rivers, Lakes, Reservoirs — Examples: Carp, Catfish

Problems in Oceans & Fisheries
Overfishing & Bycatch

Overfishing: Fishing beyond reproductive capacity → Decline in fish stocks, Biodiversity loss

Bycatch: Accidental capture of Dolphins, Sharks, Sea turtles → Death of endangered species

Marine Pollution & Climate Change
Plastic wasteOil spillsSewageIndustrial discharge

Ocean acidification: CO₂ dissolved → Harms marine organisms, Damages shell-forming species

Deforestation

Large-scale removal of forests for non-forest uses.

Causes
Agricultural ExpansionLoggingUrbanizationMining
Effects
  • Loss of Biodiversity (species lose habitats)
  • Soil Erosion (top fertile soil removed)
  • Water Cycle Disruption
  • Increased CO₂ levels (less carbon absorption)
Example

Amazon Rainforest deforestation affects global climate.

Control Measures
Afforestation & Reforestation

Afforestation: Planting trees where none existed before.

Reforestation: Replanting trees in deforested areas.

Other Measures
  • Sustainable Forest Management
  • Environmental Laws (government regulations)
  • Public Awareness & Community participation

Integrated

Features of Goal-Based Development
  • Clear Objectives: Specific goals defined before implementation
  • Time-Bound Action Plans: Fixed timeline for completion
  • Measurable Indicators: Progress monitored using data
  • Accountability: Authorities responsible for outcomes
  • Monitoring & Evaluation: Continuous assessment of progress

Principles of Good Governance

Good Governance is the effective management of public affairs and resources for the welfare of society.

Memory Trick

PTA-RREEC — Participation · Transparency · Accountability · Rule of Law · Responsiveness · Equity & Inclusiveness · Effectiveness & Efficiency · Consensus-Oriented

1. Participation

Citizens participate in decision-making.

Example

Public consultation before city development projects.

2. Transparency

Government decisions must be open and clear.

Example

Publishing budget information publicly.

3. Accountability

Officials must answer for their actions.

Example

Audits and performance reviews.

4. Rule of Law

Laws must be fairly applied to everyone.

Example

Equal legal treatment of all citizens.

5. Responsiveness

Institutions must respond quickly to public needs.

Example

Emergency services.

6. Equity & Inclusiveness

Equal opportunities for all.

Example

Accessible education and healthcare.

7. Effectiveness & Efficiency

Maximum output with minimum resource wastage.

Example

Digital governance reducing paperwork.

8. Consensus-Oriented

Decisions should consider different viewpoints.

Example

Stakeholder discussions before major policies.
